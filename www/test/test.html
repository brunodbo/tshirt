Why The U.S. Leads The World In Cotton Exports

You can grow cotton anywhere there's access to sun and water. You can grow it in places where land and labor are cheap. You can grow it in places that are close to the countries — Colombia, Indonesia, Bangladesh — where the Planet Money T-shirt was made. Yet most of the cotton in the Planet Money T-shirts was grown in the U.S.

As it turns out, this is pretty common. In fact, the U.S. exports more cotton than any country in the world.

Here's why.

Technology

The U.S. has perfected cotton by treating it as a high-tech product.

Genetically modified crops are controversial, but not among U.S. cotton farmers: TK percent of U.S. cotton comes from seeds that are constantly tweaked to produce more cotton and better resist pests.

When it's time to harvest those seeds, U.S. farmers use huge, high-tech machines that allow them to pick huge amounts of cotton in a short amount of time. Just last year, Bowen Flowers bought five John Deere Tks — at a cost of $600,000 a pop.

Like the seeds, the Tks get more productive every year. The newest John Deere picker needs just one guy to do what took 5 guys a couple years ago. One driver can do a hundred acres a day — and he barely needs to touch the steering wheel. The John Deere is, essentially, a robot that feels the cotton plants and knows the exact dimensions of the field.

Today, a single machine can pick more in a day than a picker 50 years ago could have picked in a lifetime.

Subsidies

U.S. cotton farmers benefit from a bunch of government subsidy programs. (Other countries subsidize their cotton farmers as well, by the way.) The government gives taxpayer money directly to cotton farmers, and subsidizes insurance that protects farmers if their harvest is weak or cotton prices fall.

How The U.S. Government Subsidizes Cotton Farmers

Crop Insurance
The government subsidizes several kinds of insurance available to cotton farmers. One popular option is revenue insurance, which pays farmers if they make less money than they expect. This can happen for a few reasons — cotton prices may fall, or a farmer may have a bad harvest. Whatever the reason, revenue insurance. The insurance is sold by private companies, which are subsidized by the government. The government pays a big chunk of the premiums for the farmers.
Direct Payments When Prices Fall
Through the "countercyclical program," the federal government pays cotton farmers when the average price of cotton falls below a certain level. The level is based on average prices over the past few years. Payments have been rising recently, so the government hasn't been making payments via this program.
Direct Payments When Prices Don't Fall (And, Also, When They Do)
If you grew cotton on your farm in the 1990s, you get a cash payment from the government — even if you don't grow cotton on your farm anymore, and even if cotton prices are going through the roof. The payment is based on how many acres of cotton you cultivated in the past.

The latest version of the farm bill, which Congress is working on this month is likely to change these subsidies. Farmers may get less money in the form of direct payments, and more in the form of subsidized insurance.

Data

For the Planet Money men's T-shirt, U.S. cotton traveled TK thousand miles to Indonesia, where it was spun into yarn. (And by yarn we mean thread. In the clothing business, people call it yarn.)

In Indonesia, we learned that U.S. cotton so desirable in part because factories know exactly what they're getting without ever seeing or touching the yarn. The spinners have a special recipe for their yarn, and they need exactly the right cotton. No surprises.

The U.S. is great at delivering no-surprises cotton. Every year, the USDA tests all 17 million bales of cotton harvested every year in the U.S. The farmers pay for the testing, and the USDA figures out exactly how fine, long, strong and stretchy every bale of cotton is.

The testing means a cotton buyer in Indonesia can order a bale of U.S. cotton that's guaranteed to match his exact specifications, every time.

Machines

Robots text

We chased our bales of Mississippi cotton through the streets of Jakarta and out into the Indonesian countryside, where rice fields have been turned into Honda plants and satellite farms.

It's a busy, crowded place — until you walk into the Indorama yarn factory. The building is a big as a football field, and there's almost no one there. Just row after row of shiny machines

Spinning cotton into yarn (it looks like thread, but it's called yarn) is one of the oldest technologies there is. Yarn spinning machines were a key part of the industrial revolution, 200 years ago in England. You can even spin yarn by hand, at home, if you have some cotton and a bunch of free time.

Today, yarn spinning is a high-tech, capital intensive business. The Indonesian factory where the cotton for the Planet Money men's T-shirt was spun into yarn cost $35 million. What you get for that money is a factory that can make yarn with incredible precision, 24 hours a day, 365 days a year.

The Planet Money T-shirt has six miles of yarn in it. If any of that yarn is too thick or too thin — even by a little bit — the T-shirt will warp or won't fit right. The Jockey Corporation picked out the yarn for our T-shirt — and they wouldn't tell us exactly what the specs are.

"That's our special sauce," Marion TK, Jockey's TK, said. It's like the secret formula for Coke, but softer on the skin.

The Indorama factory in Indonesia isn't using some sort of proprietary process. Anyone can buy these machines and set up a factory exactly like this. But Indonesia is in a sweet spot right in the middle of the global t-shirt trade. Wages are lower here than they are in developed countries. At the same time, unlike many countries where labor is even cheaper, Indonesia the cheap, reliable electricity and educated workforce that you need to keep high-tech factories humming.

But staying in the sweet spot in a global economy is almost impossible. At some point, a country that has cheaper labor or is closer to where clothes are made will find the money, the electricity and the workers to build their own, big spinning plant. Anyone who can produce the same quality yarn for a few pennies cheaper will win.

Ships

Ships text

The Planet Money men's T-shirt traveled around the world on its way to you. The cotton was grown in the U.S., spun into yarn in Indonesia, and sewn into shirts Bangladesh. Then a ship took the shirts back to the U.S., bound for a printing factory in Brooklyn.

All this travel was made possible by one simple innovation: the shipping container.

The Shipping Container

[ Graphic: PHOTO/GIF OF A SHIPPING CONTAINER, SHOWING STANDARD SIZE? ]

Source: ???

Big metal boxes might not seem like an innovation. But without the humble container, the global economy wouldn't be nearly as global as it is — and the Planet Money T-shirt wouldn't have been made the way it was.

Before containers, it was common for a single ship to be loaded with hundreds of thousands of different things — bananas, fish meal, steel pipes — all randomly stuffed into sacks and boxes. Workers had to pack each bag and box into the ship individually.

Even with 50 or 100 people working, it could take weeks to unload and reload a single ship.

From The Archives

[ Historical video of cotton being rolled into a ship? ]

Source: ???

Malcolm Mclean, who led the move to containers, had no experiencewith ships at all. Mclean owned a trucking company in TK in the 1950s, before the Interstate highway system was built. It took forever for his trucks to get through traffic up and down the east coast.

Mclean had an idea: What if he put his trucks onto the ship in New York, and sailed them down the coast to North Carolina? He could hop over all that traffic! Then, he had another idea: a truck is basically an engine attached to a box — so why not just make the box detachable?

Cranes could move the boxes from the trucks to the ship in New York. Cranes could put the boxes back on trucks in North Carolina. Not only would he skip traffic; it would make it profoundly easier to load and unload ships. (For much more on this story, see The Box, by TK.)

Longshoremen fought containers for years: They saw that, with cranes and standardized containers, the number of people needed to load and unload ships would plummet. In the end, shipping companies agreed to pay into a fund that would compensate workers who lost their jobs.

Standardized containers took off around the world, and by 1966 Malcolm Mclean — the guy who dreamed of putting trucks on boats to avoid traffic — had put together a fleet of container ships.

The age of containers had begun, and it greatly reduced the cost of shipping. A global distribution system emerged around standardized boxes that could be easily moved from ships, to, trains to trucks.
