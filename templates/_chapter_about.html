The idea seemed so simple: Let’s make a T-shirt, report on every facet of its creation, and sell the final product to our listeners.

The simplest shirt is touched by people and machines all over the planet — from highly paid researchers in seed laboratories to factory workers who earn a few dollars a day. By making our own t-shirt, we figured, we would discover this massive, interconnected, largely hidden world.

At first, we tried to go it alone. We went to Texas looking for a few bales of cotton. That went fine. But when visited the North Carolina mills where cotton is spun into yarn, we got laughed out of the room. We were way too small; no one spins a few bales at a time. Our approach, we realized, was a little like deciding to make a laptop from scratch, and starting by mining our own copper.

Eventually, we sought the advice of experts. Marion Smith, the director of global sourcing for Jockey, the underwear company, was patient with us on the phone. He gently corrected our mistakes. (T-shirts are knitted, not woven. They’re made from something that looks like thread, but is called yarn.) Eventually, Jockey agreed to sell us T-shirts — and, more importantly, to let us follow the creation of those shirts.

The Planet Money T-shirts are made of fabric that Jockey’s textile experts developed. They’re based on a design pattern Jockey uses for its own t-shirt line. Jockey introduced us to their suppliers in Bangladesh and Colombia, and those suppliers agreed to assemble our shirts. And Jockey designers helped us pick out the colors for our shirts.

But Jockey couldn’t tell us how many shirts to make, or how we could pay for them. For that, we turned to Kickstarter — and promised T-shirts to everyone who pledged $25. Our goal was to raise enough money for 2,000 shirts. In the end, we raised enough for 25,000. (Thanks again to everyone who ordered a shirt. Really. Amazing.)

On TK, 2013, we placed our order: TK men’s shirts, TK women’s shirts. And then we went all over the world to report on our shirt’s construction.
